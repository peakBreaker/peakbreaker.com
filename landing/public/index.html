Data Policies

Foreword

In the interest of transparency, I am writing down my thoughts in working with data in organization and implementing policies for data. These are the organization policies I am likely to promote, should you ever work with me. I, perhaps, differ in my opinion on office politics from what many engineers I've encountered argue - its not all bad - and effective politics can make the difference from terrible organizations to those that reach the skies.

Before moving on, I want to note that my loyalty is with the team I am on, and will not act as a loose cannon if I am working as an engineer.

Driven by data

To a greater and greater extent our world, and your organization, is driven by data. If you do not have a strategy in place for getting value from your data - I recommend setting up one as soon as possible.

Surveys by Gartner indicate that most big data projects fail to reach production

Data Engineering Teams

Handling data can get very complex, and traditional approaches to handling data breaks down when the data is too large. Your traditional developer team will most likely fail when you wish to do analytics very large datasets. Most big data projects fail, and it can often be boiled down to the team being set up for failure.

My opinion on this subject is heavily influenced by Jesse Anderson's book - I recommend giving it a read!

Handling big data requires certain skillsets, and a good big data team should have good foundations in the following subjects:

  • Programming Knowledge (Especially in Java, Scala and/or Python)
  • QA
  • Distributed systems
  • Big data tools (such as Hadoop, Spark, Kafka)
  • Visual and verbal communication
  • Databases & SQL
  • Basic ML (if the team is working with a Data Science applications)

On this basis, I will promote the following policies:

  • Hire to cover all basis of competence for data teams(above)
  • Have a project veteran on the team

Data Science

More and more, organizations are hiring Data Scientists to provide value and insights from their data. 'Data Scientists', in general, are people with a background in statistics, probability, or some form of academics. They may be PhD level employees, and have a heavy theoretical backing. On the other hand 'Data Engineers' or 'Software Engieers' have a background in engineering and development, and often have more experience and interest in programming, infrastructure and tools.

If tasked with doing engineering, Data Scientists will implement the system in a longer time with less quality, and they will become unhappy.

On this basis, I will promote the following policies:

  • Have data infrastructure in place before hiring data scientists if possible
  • Have every Data Scientist backed up with 2-6 engineers in the organization

Working with Data Technologies

Working with technology itself often has large risks. Project deadlines are frequently broken, and once something goes wrong in technology - costs may skyrocket.
